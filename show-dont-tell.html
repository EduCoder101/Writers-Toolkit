Show, Don't Tell

Paint pictures with your words.

What does "Show, Don't Tell" mean?

When we write, it's tempting to just tell the reader what happened: "He was scared." "She was happy." But interesting writers do something different. They describe the action so the reader can picture it for themselves.

We call this building mind pictures. Instead of telling the reader how to feel, we give them enough detail to feel it on their own.

Compare these two versions:

Telling

Daniel saw the monster and was scared, so he ran away as fast as possible.

Showing

Daniel felt a chill down his spine as he saw the monster. The feeling was familiar. It was fear. He knew what he had to do. He nearly slipped as he began to run away as fast as he could.

The "telling" version gives us facts. The "showing" version slows down the moment so we can feel what Daniel feels.

Technique 1: Add sensory details

👁

What can the character see, hear, feel, smell, or taste? The five senses are your most powerful tool.

Telling

He walked inside. It was dark and dusty.

Showing

He stepped through the doorway and the cold hit him immediately(touch). The air smelled of damp wood and something rotten(smell). Dust floated in the thin beam of light from the crack in the door(sight). Somewhere deep inside, water dripped steadily onto stone(sound).

Four senses in four sentences. Each one helps the reader feel like they are standing in that doorway.

Technique 2: Show thoughts and reactions

🧠

Instead of "he was scared", show us what fear looks like: trembling, freezing, a racing heart. Instead of "it was old", show the character thinking about what they notice.

Telling

The stairs were old.

Showing

Creaking and sagging under his weight, Andrew knew the stairs must be very old.

Technique 3: Stretch the moment

⏲

A single second of action can become a whole paragraph. The more important the moment, the more you slow it down.

Telling

The door closed behind him.

Showing

He jumped as with a loud thud the door closed behind him. He froze. Fear surged through his body. He hadn't touched it. How had it closed?
