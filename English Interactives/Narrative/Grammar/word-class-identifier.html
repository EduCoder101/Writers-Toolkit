Nouns

A noun is a word that names a person, place, thing or idea.

Click to see examples...

The dolphin leapt through the waves. (thing)
Courage is hard to find. (abstract noun: an idea)
We visited Sydney last summer. (place)
My sister plays guitar. (person)
She hid her sadness behind a smile. (abstract noun: a feeling)
Nouns include ideas and feelings (abstract nouns like courage, sadness, freedom), not just things you can touch.
✍ Write this in your book:
Nouns = a word that names a person, place, thing or idea.
Abstract nouns name ideas or feelings you cannot touch (e.g. courage, sadness, freedom).
Write one example of each: person, place, thing, abstract noun (idea), abstract noun (feeling).

Verbs

A verb is a word that shows an action, a state or an occurrence.

Click to see examples...

The cheetah sprinted across the plain. (action)
She is the fastest runner. (state of being)
The earthquake happened at dawn. (occurrence)
Every sentence must have at least one verb. If there is no verb, it is not a sentence.

Adjectives

An adjective is a word that describes or modifies a noun. It tells you what kind, which one, or how many.

Click to see examples...

The enormous → whale surfaced beside our tiny → boat.

The arrows show which noun each adjective describes.

She wore a red → jacket.
Three → koalas sat in the tree.
An adjective always connects to a noun. Ask yourself: which noun is this word describing?

Conjunctions

A conjunction is a word that joins words, phrases or clauses together.

Click to see the two types...

Coordinating Conjunctions (FANBOYS)

For   And   Nor   But   Or   Yet   So

Subordinating Conjunctions

because   although   when   if   while   since   until   after   before
I wanted to swim but the water was freezing.
Although it rained, we still played outside.
Conjunctions are the glue of sentences. They hold ideas together.

Adverbs

An adverb is a word that modifies (gives more information about) a verb, an adjective or another adverb. It often tells you how, when, where or to what extent.

Click to explore the four types...

How (manner)

The cat crept silently ← modifies the verb "crept" across the room.

When (time)

We will leave tomorrow ← modifies the verb "leave".

Where (place)

Please sit here ← modifies the verb "sit".

To what extent (degree)

She was extremely → modifies the adjective "tired" tired.
✍ Write this in your book:
Adverbs = a word that modifies a verb, an adjective or another adverb. It tells you how, when, where or to what extent.
Four types: How (manner), When (time), Where (place), To what extent (degree).
Write one example sentence for each type. Underline the adverb and draw an arrow to the word it modifies.

The Adverb Trap: Not All Adverbs End in -ly

Many adverbs end in -ly: quickly, carefully, silently. But lots do not.

Click to see the list...

soon   very   never   always   often   here   there   now   then   almost   already   also   just   still   quite   too   well   fast   hard   late   yesterday   today

Tricky: Same word, different class

Some words can be adjectives OR adverbs depending on their job.

She is a fast → runner.
ADJECTIVE: describes the noun "runner"
She runs fast.
ADVERB: modifies the verb "runs"
That was a hard → question.
ADJECTIVE: describes the noun "question"
She worked hard.
ADVERB: modifies the verb "worked"
To decide if a word is an adverb, do not look at the word. Look at its job.
Ask: is this word telling me more about a verb, an adjective or another adverb? If yes, it is an adverb.
✍ Write this in your book:
Adverbs that do not end in -ly: soon, very, never, always, often, here, there, now, then, almost, already, also, just, still, quite, too, well, fast, hard, late, yesterday, today.
Write two sentences where the same word is used first as an adjective and then as an adverb (like the "fast" example).

The Six Word Classes

Noun

Names a person, place, thing or idea.

dolphin, courage, Sydney

Verb

Shows an action, state or occurrence.

sprinted, is, happened

Adjective

Describes or modifies a noun.

enormous, red, three

Adverb

Modifies a verb, adjective or adverb.

silently, tomorrow, very

Pronoun

Takes the place of a noun.

she, them, our

Conjunction

Joins words, phrases or clauses.

but, because, although

Go Deeper: Add Adverbs

Good writers use adverbs to add detail and precision. Read this plain passage, then see how adverbs bring it to life.

Worked Example

Before: The dog sat on the mat. It looked at the door. The handle turned.

Click to see the improved version...

After: The dog sat patiently on the mat. It looked hopefully at the door. Slowly, the handle turned.
Three adverbs were added: patiently (how the dog sat), hopefully (how it looked), and Slowly (how the handle turned). Each one adds a detail the reader can picture.
