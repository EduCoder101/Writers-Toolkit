Learning Intention & Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to identify and construct simple, compound and complex sentences so we can vary our sentence types in writing.

Success Criteria

  • I can identify a simple sentence (one independent clause).
  • I can identify a compound sentence (two independent clauses joined by a coordinating conjunction).
  • I can identify a complex sentence (an independent clause + a dependent clause).
  • I can write my own simple, compound and complex sentences.

Take flat, boring paragraphs and bring them to life by mixing sentence types. Then tackle the challenges below to sharpen your skills even further.

Creating and Evaluating
Each passage below is made up entirely of simple sentences. Rewrite it using a mix of simple, compound and complex sentences. You must use at least one of each type.

Conjunction cheat sheet

Coordinating conjunctions (for compound sentences)

Remember FANBOYS: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So

These join two independent clauses (clauses that could each stand alone).

Example: "The dog barked at the postman, but the cat slept through the whole thing."
Two complete thoughts joined by "but". Either clause works on its own.
Example: "We could walk to school, or we could take the bus."
Two options, each a full sentence, joined by "or".
Subordinating conjunctions (for complex sentences)

because, although, when, if, while, since, after, before, until, unless, as, whenever

These turn a clause into a dependent clause (one that cannot stand alone).

Example: "Although the rain was heavy, the children played outside."
"Although the rain was heavy" cannot stand alone. It depends on the second clause.
Example: "The park was empty because the gates had been locked early."
"Because" creates a dependent clause explaining why. Notice: no comma needed when the dependent clause comes second.
Quick tip

Not every sentence needs to be compound or complex. Keeping some simple sentences in your paragraph gives it punch. Short sentences after long ones create contrast and grab the reader's attention.

The storm hit the coast at midnight. The waves crashed against the seawall. The power went out across the town. People huddled in their homes. Emergency workers drove through the flooded streets.
Model response

[COMPLEX] When the storm hit the coast at midnight, the waves crashed against the seawall and the power went out across the town. [COMPOUND]

People huddled in their homes. [SIMPLE: short sentence creates tension]

[COMPLEX] Although emergency workers drove through the flooded streets, they struggled to reach everyone in time.

Self-assessment: Did you include at least one simple, one compound and one complex sentence? Does mixing sentence types make your paragraph flow better? Which type created the most impact?
The crowd filled the stadium. The players ran onto the pitch. The whistle blew. The home team passed the ball up the field. A striker shot at goal. The goalkeeper dived. The ball hit the net. The crowd roared.
Model response

[COMPLEX] As the crowd filled the stadium, the players ran onto the pitch and the atmosphere crackled with excitement. [COMPOUND]

The whistle blew. [SIMPLE: punchy, marks the moment]

[COMPLEX] After the home team passed the ball up the field, a striker struck a powerful shot at goal.

The goalkeeper dived, but the ball hit the net. [COMPOUND: "but" builds suspense]

The crowd roared. [SIMPLE: short and powerful]

Self-assessment: Notice how the simple sentences ("The whistle blew." and "The crowd roared.") land with more impact because they follow longer sentences. Did your rewrite use short sentences at key moments too?
Writer's thinking: You are composing with purpose. You are choosing which sentence type to use and where, to create rhythm, tension and flow.
Challenge: Build tension with rhythm
Professional writers often use a pattern of two short simple sentences followed by a longer compound or complex sentence to create tension. The short sentences build pace. Then the longer sentence releases it.
Example of the technique:
"The door creaked open. The hallway was dark. Although she wanted to turn back, something pulled her forward and she stepped inside."
Simple. Simple. Complex-compound. The two short sentences create urgency, then the long sentence slows the pace and adds detail.
Your turn: Write a short scene (3 to 5 sentences) using this short-short-long pattern. Choose your own topic, or use one of these: a sports final, a thunderstorm, being lost in a forest.
Model response (thunderstorm theme)

The sky went black. The air stood still. [SIMPLE + SIMPLE: pace quickens]

Before the first raindrop hit the ground, a crack of thunder split the sky and the trees bent sideways in the wind. [COMPLEX-COMPOUND: long sentence releases the tension]

Nobody moved. [SIMPLE: snaps back to tension]

Notice how alternating between short and long sentences creates a rhythm that mirrors the drama of the scene. The simple sentences feel like held breath; the longer sentence feels like the storm breaking.

Writer's thinking: You are controlling pace. Sentence length is a tool. Short sentences speed things up and build urgency. Longer sentences slow the reader down and add detail. Alternating between them creates rhythm.
Challenge: Cut the clutter
Sometimes writers try so hard to make their writing fancy that it becomes confusing and hard to follow. Good writers know when to keep things simple. Below are some sentences that have been over-complicated. Rewrite each one more clearly, using simpler sentence structures. You might turn one long sentence into two or three shorter ones.

Sentence 1:

Although the extremely ferocious and incredibly terrifying dragon, which had been sleeping in the cave for what seemed like an eternity of countless years, suddenly woke up because of the noise that the knights, who were not being very quiet at all, had been making while they crept cautiously through the dark and gloomy tunnel, it did not immediately attack them.

Sentence 2:

Because the weather, which had been sunny and warm and really quite pleasant for the whole entire morning, suddenly changed when dark clouds rolled in from the west and the temperature dropped significantly and dramatically, the children, who had been happily playing in the park and having a wonderful time, had to quickly and hurriedly pack up all of their things and run back home.
Model responses

Sentence 1 rewritten:

"The dragon had been sleeping in the cave for years. [SIMPLE] When the knights crept noisily through the tunnel, it woke up. [COMPLEX] Strangely, it did not attack. [SIMPLE]"

Three sentences instead of one. The meaning is the same, but each idea gets its own space. Notice how "Strangely, it did not attack" is more dramatic as a standalone sentence than when it was buried at the end of a monster clause.

Sentence 2 rewritten:

"The morning had been warm and sunny, but dark clouds rolled in from the west. [COMPOUND] As the temperature dropped, the children packed up their things and ran home. [COMPLEX]"

The original said "quickly and hurriedly" (which mean the same thing) and "really quite pleasant" (which adds nothing). Strong writing removes words that repeat ideas or add no meaning. Two sentences here do the job better than one long one.

Writer's thinking: You are editing for clarity. A common mistake is thinking longer sentences are better sentences. Skilled writers use complex sentences when they need them, not to show off. If a sentence is confusing on first read, it probably needs to be split up or trimmed down.
Bonus challenge
Can you write a compound-complex sentence? That is a sentence with at least two independent clauses AND at least one dependent clause.
Example compound-complex sentence

"When the power went out [dependent], the town was plunged into darkness [independent 1] and families searched for torches and candles [independent 2]."

This has everything: a dependent clause ("when..."), and two independent clauses joined by "and". Not required at Stage 3, but a powerful tool for advanced writers.

Writer's thinking: You are extending your toolkit. Compound-complex sentences let you pack multiple ideas into a single sentence.
