Learning Intention

We are learning to identify and use simple, compound, complex and fragment sentences, and to understand how sentence structure affects the tone and meaning of our writing.

Success Criteria
  • I can identify the four sentence types (simple, compound, complex, fragment) and explain the structure of each.
  • I can classify sentences from a text by their structure and explain my reasoning.
  • I can rewrite a sentence by changing its structure and explain how the meaning or tone shifts.
  • I can use a deliberate mix of sentence types in my own writing for effect.

Same story, different feel

Read these two versions of the same moment. What feels different?

Version A
Alfred walked along the track. He had a swag on his back. He had fought in a war. Nobody remembered him. He did not get a parade. He did not get a medal. He kept walking.
Version B
Although no one had asked him to, Alfred kept walking. He walked the tracks, and his swag bit into his shoulders with every step. No one remembered him. Not really. Old boots. A battered hat. A folded photo he never showed anyone. He had fought for a country that did not fight for him.

Version A uses only simple sentences. Every sentence follows the same pattern: subject, verb, done. It reads like a list of facts.

Version B mixes all four types: complex ("Although no one had asked him to, Alfred kept walking"), compound ("He walked the tracks, and his swag bit into his shoulders"), simple ("No one remembered him"), and fragment ("Old boots. A battered hat.").

The variety controls the rhythm. The fragments force the reader to slow down. The complex sentence at the end carries the emotional weight. That is what sentence structure can do.

Simple Sentence

One independent clause
A sentence with one complete idea. It has a subject and a verb, and it makes sense on its own.
The sun was shining.
One subject + one verb = one complete idea.

Simple does not mean short or boring. This is also a simple sentence:

The tall, grey-haired soldier walked slowly along the empty, dusty road.

It is still one idea with one subject (the soldier) and one verb (walked). The extra detail comes from adjectives and adverbial phrases, not from additional clauses.

Compound Sentence

Two independent clauses joined by a conjunction
Two complete ideas connected by a coordinating conjunction (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so). Each clause could be its own sentence.
It was raining, but we went outside.
Remember FANBOYS: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So.

Quick test: cover one clause. Does the other still make sense as a complete sentence? If yes, it is a compound sentence.

"It was raining." Complete. "We went outside." Complete. Both are independent clauses.

Complex Sentence

Independent clause + dependent clause
One main clause that makes sense on its own, combined with a dependent clause that cannot stand alone. They are joined by a subordinating conjunction (although, because, when, if, since, while, after, before, until).
Although it was late, she kept working.
Dependent clause = cannot stand alone. Independent clause = makes sense on its own.

The dependent clause can go at the start or the end:

She kept working although it was late.

When the dependent clause comes first, we use a comma after it. When it comes second, no comma is needed.

Fragment

An incomplete sentence, used for effect
A fragment is missing a subject, a verb, or both. In everyday writing, this is an error. But in narrative and creative writing, authors use fragments deliberately for emphasis, rhythm, or emotion.
Too tired to continue.
Missing a subject. Who was too tired? The author leaves it unsaid.

Fragments create impact through what they leave out:

"Old boots. A battered hat. A folded photo."
Three fragments in a row. No verbs. No actions. Just objects. The stillness forces the reader to pause and feel the weight of each item.

Rewrite challenge

Take this simple sentence and transform it three different ways. After each rewrite, reveal the model to compare.

Original (simple)
"He walked the tracks, his swag on his back."
Compound Join two independent clauses with a FANBOYS conjunction

Both halves must be able to stand alone as complete sentences.

He walked the tracks, and his swag bit deeper into his shoulders with every step.

Two complete ideas joined by "and". Each clause could be its own sentence. The compound structure gives the sentence an even, steady rhythm, as though one fact follows the other without emotion.

Complex Add a dependent clause using a subordinating conjunction

Try starting with although, because, even though, while, or when. The dependent clause cannot stand alone.

Although his legs ached and the sun pressed down on him, he kept walking the tracks with his swag on his back.

The dependent clause ("Although his legs ached...") adds context and builds empathy. It tells us he is suffering, which makes the simple act of walking feel like determination. The reader now understands why this matters.

Fragment Break it into fragments. Remove the subject, the verb, or both.

Fragments are incomplete on purpose. Think about what you leave out and what that makes the reader feel.

Walking. Always walking. Swag on his back, dust in his boots, sun on his neck.

No subject. No main verb. Just images stacked together. The repetition of "walking" creates a sense of endless monotony, and the list of details ("dust in his boots, sun on his neck") puts the reader right inside the moment. Fragments slow the reader down and force them to feel each detail separately.
