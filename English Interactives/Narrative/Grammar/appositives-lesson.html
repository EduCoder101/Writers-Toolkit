Learning Intention

We are learning to use appositives to add depth, detail and description to our writing.

Success Criteria

  • I can explain what an appositive is and how it works in a sentence.
  • I can identify appositives in sentences and explain what detail they add about the noun.
  • I can write sentences that include effective appositives using a variety of starters (a/an, the, who, with, known for).
  • I can add appositives to a paragraph to enrich the detail for the reader.

Hook Paragraph

Mrs Frisby climbed onto the rock, her small body trembling in the morning chill. She gazed out over the garden, which seemed unusually quiet. Timothy, her youngest child, lay in the burrow behind her, too sick to move. The medicine he needed was out of reach, and the plough would soon arrive. The rats, a mysterious group who lived beneath the rose bush, might be her only hope. Their home, a maze of tunnels lit with tiny bulbs, remained hidden from sight.
Discussion
What do you notice about this paragraph? What extra details does it give us about the characters and places?
Notice
The highlighted phrases give us extra information about a noun right next to it. These are called appositives.

What is an Appositive?

An appositive is a noun or noun phrase that adds extra information about another noun right next to it.

Timothy, her youngest child, lay in the burrow.
The noun
Timothy is the noun we want to know more about.
The appositive
her youngest child is the appositive. It renames or describes Timothy, telling us who he is.
Tip: The appositive often sits between commas, but not always. It can also come at the end of a sentence before a full stop.

Why Use Appositives?

Adds detail and description without needing a separate sentence.
Helps paint a clearer picture for the reader.
Helps introduce or explain characters, settings, or objects.
Without
The rats might be her only hope.
With appositive
The rats, a mysterious group who lived beneath the rose bush, might be her only hope.

What Can Appositives Tell Us? (People)

Tap each sentence to reveal what kind of detail the appositive provides about the character.

Luna, a grey tabby cat, slept on the windowsill.
description Tells us what Luna looks like.
Daniel, a kind and curious boy, asked many questions.
personality Tells us what Daniel is like as a person.
Mrs Chen, a retired scientist, volunteered at the library.
role / background Tells us about her career and life stage.
James, his best friend, cheered him on.
relationship Tells us how James is connected to another character.
Leo, born in the Blue Mountains, knew the terrain well.
origin / background Tells us where Leo comes from.
Rosie, tired and sore, sat down to rest.
physical / emotional state Tells us how Rosie is feeling right now.
Noah, only eight years old, led the group.
age Tells us how old Noah is, which makes his action more surprising.

What Can Appositives Tell Us? (Places and Things)

Tap each sentence to reveal the type of detail.

The cave, a dark and narrow tunnel, echoed with dripping water.
description Tells us what the cave looks like.
The building, once a post office, now houses a museum.
history Tells us what the building used to be.
The island, lush and overgrown, sat untouched by time.
description Tells us the appearance and atmosphere of the island.
The compass, a tool for finding direction, lay on the table.
purpose Tells us what the compass does.
The town, over 200 years old, still held secrets.
age / time Tells us how old the town is.

Non-Examples of Appositives

Not every extra phrase is an appositive. Look at these and think about why they do not work.

Not an appositive
Mrs Frisby, who likes cheese, went to the rose bush.
Why not?
This detail is irrelevant to the story. A good appositive adds information that helps the reader understand the character, setting, or plot.
Not an appositive
The cat, it was grey, scratched the post.
Why not?
This is a full clause (it has a subject and verb), not a noun phrase. It creates a clunky, confusing sentence. A correct version would be: The cat, a grey tabby, scratched the post.
Key points: A good appositive should be relevant to the story, rename or describe the noun directly, and not be a full clause or sentence crammed in with commas.

Word Bank for Appositive Starters

Here are some common ways to begin an appositive. Keep this in mind for your writing tasks.

a / an ...
Leo, a clever inventor, built his own robot.
the ...
Noah, the youngest of four, was full of questions.
who ...
Mia, who loved to read, finished five books in a week.
with ...
Sarah, with a red ribbon in her hair, skipped down the hallway.
known for ...
Jack, known for his courage, stepped forward.
including ...
Emma brought her pets, including her pet parrot, to the show.
like ...
The girl, like a fearless explorer, climbed the rocky hill.
such as ...
He admired artists, such as Leonardo da Vinci, for their detail.

I Do: Teacher Model

Lily walked into the classroom.
Step 1: Think
Who is Lily? What might the reader want to know about her?
Step 2: Ask
What detail fits the context of this sentence? If she is walking into a classroom, perhaps she is a new student.
With appositive
Lily, a new student from Brisbane, walked into the classroom.
Notice
The appositive a new student from Brisbane tells us two things: she is new, and where she is from. It sits between commas, right next to the noun it describes (Lily).

We Do: Shared Example

The creature scuttled under the table.
Think together
What kind of creature is it? What does it look like? What detail would help the reader picture it?
Modelled version
The creature, a hairy, three-eyed beetle, scuttled under the table.
Notice
The appositive tells us what the creature is and what it looks like. The commas signal where the appositive starts and ends.

Before and After: Appositives in a Paragraph

Read this paragraph. It gives the basic outline of a scene, but it lacks detail.

A girl stood at the edge of the field. She was holding a bag. Behind her, the trees moved in the wind. In the distance, a building stood near a fence. The girl began to walk. After a few minutes, she saw someone sitting on a bench. She stopped and waited.

Now see what happens when appositives are added:

A girl, a quiet traveller with a curious mind, stood at the edge of the field. She was holding a bag, a battered canvas satchel covered in patches. Behind her, the trees, tall and swaying like sleepy dancers, moved in the wind. In the distance stood a building, an old weatherboard shed with a rusted roof, just beyond a crooked fence. The girl began to walk, her footsteps soft on the narrow path. After a few minutes, she saw someone sitting on a bench, a boy, no older than she was, with a notebook in his hands. She stopped and waited, unsure whether to speak or turn back.
Discussion
What difference do the appositives make? How many can you count? What types of detail do they add (description, age, appearance, purpose)?

Your Turn: Rewrite a Paragraph

Choose one of these paragraphs and rewrite it with at least three appositives that help the reader picture the scene.

Option A: The Lost Item
A girl walked across the school playground. She knelt beside the bench and picked something up. She turned it over in her hand. A friend called her from the basketball court. She walked over to show what she had found.
Option B: The Discovery
The child stepped into the room. It was quiet. A table stood in the middle. On top of it was a box. The child walked over and opened the lid.
Option C: The Journey
The vehicle rolled down the road. The driver stared ahead. Beside them sat another person. They did not speak. The road curved towards the mountains.
Before you write, think about the nouns in your chosen paragraph. What could you tell the reader about each character, object, or place? Choose detail that is relevant and interesting.
Your rewrite
Model response (Option A)
A girl, a quiet Year 5 student with untied shoelaces, walked across the school playground. She knelt beside the bench, a faded wooden seat with graffiti on the side, and picked something up. She turned it over in her hand, a small silver key with a crooked tooth. A friend, her classmate from netball training, called her from the basketball court. She walked over to show what she had found.

This model uses four appositives, each with a different starter. Compare yours. Did you add at least three? Do your appositives add relevant detail that helps the reader picture the scene?
