Learning Intention

We are learning to write an effective flashback scene that moves between the present and the past using a trigger, time signal and return cue.

Success Criteria

  • I can explain why writers use flashbacks and what they achieve for the reader.
  • I can identify the three ingredients of an effective flashback (trigger, time signal, return cue) in a model text.
  • I can plan a flashback scene with a clear before, flashback and after structure.
  • I can write a flashback that uses a sensory trigger, shifts tense to enter the memory, and returns smoothly to the present.

Two Story Openings

Read these two openings. What do you notice they have in common?

As Sarah stepped onto the dimly lit stage, her heart raced with anxiety. She remembered the first time she had ever performed in front of an audience.
Maya tightened her shoelaces as the race was about to begin. In a heartbeat, she was eight again, hearing the crack of the starting pistol at her first ever race.
Discussion
Both characters are in the present, but something pulls them back into a memory. The reader is taken from "now" into "then". This technique is called a flashback.
Notice
In Sarah's opening, "she remembered" is the bridge to the past. In Maya's, "in a heartbeat, she was eight again" transports us. What sensory details trigger each memory? (The stage and lights for Sarah; the sound of shoelaces and the race for Maya.)

Why Do Writers Use Flashbacks?

Flashbacks serve three core purposes in narrative writing.

Reveal Hidden Motives

Show the reader why a character acts the way they do by revealing a past experience.

Deepen Character

Give the reader a richer understanding of who the character is and what shaped them.

Foreshadow Events

Plant a clue from the past that hints at what might happen next in the story.

Three Ingredients of an Effective Flashback

Every effective flashback needs these three things to work smoothly.

Trigger

An object, sound, smell, taste or touch in the present that jolts the memory. The reader needs to feel the bridge between now and then.

Time Signal

A phrase that guides the reader backwards in time. For example: "Three winters earlier..." or "Suddenly, he was eleven again..."

Return Cue

A sentence that pulls the reader back to the present. Often uses a sensory detail from "now" to snap back: "The crowd's roar dissolved..."

Spot the Ingredients

Read this short flashback. Can you find all three ingredients?

Leo's fingers brushed the cold medal in his pocket. Five years earlier, he had stood on that podium, heart hammering, the anthem echoing through the stadium. Back then the medal felt like a ticket to glory. The crowd's roar dissolved, and the chill wind of the platform gave way to the drizzle on today's pavement.
Trigger
"Leo's fingers brushed the cold medal in his pocket." The physical touch of the medal is what jolts his memory. It is a tactile trigger.
Time signal
"Five years earlier" takes the reader backwards. Notice how the verb shifts to past perfect: "he had stood".
Return cue
"The crowd's roar dissolved, and the chill wind of the platform gave way to the drizzle on today's pavement." The word "dissolved" signals the memory fading, and "today's pavement" snaps us back to now.

The Full Flashback Structure

A complete flashback story has five parts. The trigger and return cue are the hinges that swing the door between present and past.

Before

Present moment. What is the character doing? What situation are they in?

→

Trigger

A sensory detail that jolts the memory.

→

Flashback

The memory itself. Vivid sensory detail. Past tense.

→

Return Cue

A sentence that pulls us back to the present.

→

After

The character makes a decision or takes an action because of what they remembered.

Key insight: The "before" section helps the reader care about the character. The "after" section shows how the memory changes something: a choice, a mood, or a relationship. Without the "after", the flashback is just a detour. With it, it becomes the turning point.

Controlling Tense in a Flashback

Tense is the gear stick that moves the reader between present and past. Here is the pattern.

Before (present moment): Use past tense to describe what the character is doing now.
"Leo's fingers brushed the cold medal." Simple past tense places us in the story's "now".
Entering the flashback: Shift to past perfect ("had + verb") to show we are moving further back.
"He had stood on that podium." Past perfect signals "this happened before the story's now". After a sentence or two, you can relax back to simple past within the memory.
Returning to the present: Shift back to simple past (or present tense if your story uses it) to signal we are back in "now".
"The music faded, the garage returning around us." Simple past and present participle pull us out of the memory.

After
The garage always smelled of old boxes and lawnmower petrol. I was rummaging through the shelves, looking for the tent poles, when Dad appeared behind me holding something small and dusty. It was a cassette tape, labelled in fading biro: Road Trip '95. He turned it over in his hands, smiling softly. "Haven't seen this in years." He slipped it into the old player on the bench. A crackle. Then the warm blur of guitar chords and a tinny pop beat filled the air. Suddenly, he was eleven again, squashed between his sisters in the back of their rattling Kombi van. The windows were down, hot wind tangling their hair as the bush blurred by. Grandma was in the front, passing lemonade back without turning around. The tape warbled as they all sang, off-key, full volume. At every roadhouse, Grandpa made them race to the toilet block and back before the song finished. Dad remembered that rule more than the destination. The music faded, the garage returning around us with the clink of tent poles. Dad chuckled to himself. "Best soundtrack I ever owned," he said, slipping the tape into my hand. "We should make our own this year." I looked at the crumpled label, imagining long roads, off-key singing, and lemonade in the back seat. The tent poles could wait. We had a playlist to build.
Notice
The trigger is sensory: the sight of the cassette tape and the sound of the music playing. The return cue echoes the sound fading. The "after" shows how the flashback changed something: Dad and the narrator decide to make their own road-trip playlist. The memory becomes a catalyst for action.

Mum flicks off the television and sighs as rain drums the window-panes. Holiday plans seem washed away until she opens an old photo album and pauses at a faded shot of a bright-striped deckchair on Bondi's sand. The battered photo becomes more than paper. When she was ten, she tore along that same shore, ice-cream melting down her arm while Grandpa teased her for feeding chips to greedy gulls. The sea smelt of coconut suncream and salty spray, her laughter riding the wind. They built a lopsided castle that surrendered to the evening tide, but she did not mind; the day felt endless. The photo blurs back into now, and the rain-grey lounge room comes into focus. Mum taps the picture and smiles. "You know what?" she says, eyes sparkling like they did in the snapshot. "Rain never stopped your grandpa. We went anyway." She reaches for her phone, fingers dancing. Within minutes she has booked a modest holiday cottage a few blocks from the same stretch of sand. I stare at the photo, then at Mum. The rain still hammers outside, but inside everything feels brighter. Next month I will sit in a deckchair of my own, listening to gulls screech overhead, and I will understand why a little drizzle never ruined a true beach day.
Discussion
Compare this exemplar to "The Cassette". What kind of trigger does each use? (Cassette = sound; Beach = sight/photo.) How does each "after" section show the character being changed by the memory? (Cassette = making a playlist; Beach = booking a holiday.)

Plan Your Flashback

Use the five-part structure to plan your story before you write. Jot notes in each section.

Before (present moment)

Where is your character? What are they doing? What are they thinking about? What has been going on in their life?

Trigger

What sensory detail (sight, sound, smell, taste, touch) jolts the memory? Be specific.

Flashback (the memory)

What happened in the past? What sensory details will you include? What did the character feel? Remember to use past perfect ("had + verb") to enter the memory.

Return cue

How will you snap the reader back to the present? What present-moment sensation pulls them out of the memory?

After (the change)

How does the flashback change the character? What decision do they make, or what action do they take, because of what they remembered?

Write Your Flashback Story

Using your plan, write your flashback story. Aim for at least 150 words. Remember the five-part structure and your tense shifts.
