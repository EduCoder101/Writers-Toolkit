Phase 2 of 5 · 8 min

How to punctuate dialogue

Watch how a messy exchange becomes clear, rule by rule.

"That's stupid," Con snapped. "We're not eating insects."
Fred glared at her. "Then you'll starve."
Rule 0 of 6
Rule 1: Speech marks
Put opening and closing speech marks around the exact words someone says. Everything inside the marks is spoken aloud.
Rule 2: Capital letter
The first word inside speech marks always starts with a capital letter, just like the start of any sentence.
Rule 3: Comma before closing
If the speech is followed by a reporting clause (like "Con snapped"), put a comma before the closing speech mark instead of a full stop.
Rule 4: Reporting clause
The reporting clause tells us who is speaking and how. "Con snapped" tells us it is Con and she is angry. Other examples: whispered, muttered, shouted, replied.
Rule 5: New speaker, new line
Every time a different character speaks, start a new line. This helps the reader keep track of who is talking.
Rule 6: Action beat
An action beat is a short description of what a character does, placed near their dialogue. "Fred glared at her" is an action beat. It shows us Fred's reaction without using a reporting verb.

How dialogue reveals character

The same words feel completely different depending on the reporting clause and action beat. Read both versions aloud.

Version A
"That's stupid," Con snapped.
Version B
"That's stupid," Con whispered.

"Snapped" shows Con as stubborn, dismissive, confrontational. She is challenging Fred openly.

"Whispered" shows Con as scared, unsure, vulnerable. The same words feel like a plea instead of a fight.

Readers Theatre

Ask two students to read Version A aloud, then Version B. How does their voice change? Which version creates more tension? Which creates more sympathy for Con?

Corrected version
"I can't believe we have to sleep here," Lila said, looking around the den.

"It's better than outside," Fred replied. "At least it's dry."

Con rolled her eyes. "Dry," she muttered. "There's a hole in the roof."
Check yours: Did you start a new line for each speaker? Did you put commas before reporting clauses? Did you capitalise the first word after opening speech marks? Count how many of the 6 rules you applied correctly.

Adding action beats

Action beats show what a character does instead of (or alongside) telling us how they spoke. Watch how the dialogue transforms.

Before: just words
"That's disgusting," Con said. "I'm not eating that."
"You'll have to," Fred replied.
After: action beats added
Con folded his arms. "That's disgusting." He turned away from the fire. "I'm not eating that."

Fred stepped closer, holding out the skewer. "You'll have to."

What changed: The reporting clauses ("Con said", "Fred replied") have been replaced with action beats. We can see what the characters are doing. The tension is visual now, not just verbal.

Notice: When you use an action beat instead of a reporting clause, the dialogue ends with a full stop (not a comma), because the action beat is its own sentence.

Readers Theatre

Three students: one reads the "before" version aloud, then two others perform the "after" version (one as Con, one as Fred) with the actions. How does it feel different to listen to? Which version makes you feel the tension more?

Phase 4 of 5 · 15 min

Write your own dialogue

Con and Fred are arguing about eating insects in the jungle. Write what they say.

Your task

Write a short dialogue (8 to 10 lines) between Con and Fred during the argument about food in the jungle.

Your dialogue must include:

  • Correct punctuation (speech marks, commas, capitals, new line per speaker)
  • At least two action beats
  • Clear tension between the characters

📝 Punctuation rules (tap to open)

▼
1 Speech marks around the exact words spoken.
2 Capital letter at the start of speech.
3 Comma before the closing speech mark if a reporting clause follows.
4 Reporting clause tells us who spoke and how (e.g. "Con snapped").
5 New speaker = new line.
6 Action beats show what the character does. They are separate sentences.

Your dialogue

Challenge: add a third character

Add Lila as a third character who interrupts or reacts to the argument. How does she change the dynamic? Does she take a side, or try to calm things down?

Model response
Con stared at the skewer in Fred's hand. "That is disgusting." "It's protein," Fred said, turning the grub slowly over the flame. "We haven't eaten in two days." "I don't care," Con hissed. She stepped back from the fire. "I'm not putting that in my mouth." Fred held the skewer out towards her. "Then don't." Lila appeared between them. "I'll try one," she said quietly, taking the skewer from Fred. Con watched Lila chew and swallow. Her stomach growled. "Fine." Con snatched a grub from the leaf. "But if I'm sick, it's your fault." Fred almost smiled. "Deal."
Action beats used: "Con stared at the skewer", "She stepped back from the fire", "Lila appeared between them", "Fred almost smiled". Each shows character without a reporting verb.
Tension shown through: Short sentences ("Then don't."), physical reactions (stepping back, stomach growling), and the shift from refusal to reluctant acceptance.
Character revealed: Con is stubborn but hungry. Fred is practical and patient. Lila is the peacemaker who leads by example.
