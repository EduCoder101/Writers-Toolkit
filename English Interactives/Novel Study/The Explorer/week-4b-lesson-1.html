Phase 2: Hook
Complications and Rising Action
Every great story needs a problem. Let's think about what makes that problem real for the reader.
Think about a story you know well. What is the BIG problem the main character has to overcome?
Phase 3: Hook
Key Terms
Two important concepts that drive every story forward.
Complication
The complication is the central problem a character faces in a story. It is the reason the story exists.
Rising Action
Rising action is the part of the narrative where tension builds. A series of events happens as the character tries to resolve the problem. This is what keeps us reading.

Phase 5: Hook
The Explorer: Our Novel
Now let's connect what we know about complications to the book we are reading.
What is the complication?
What is the key complication the characters in The Explorer must overcome? After the plane crashes in the jungle, the children must survive without adults and without resources.
Every great story has a central complication and a rising action that builds tension. Today we will look at one of the tools authors use to make that tension feel real on the page: physical verbs.

Phase 8: I Do
Modelling on 'Flight'
Let's look at how Katherine Rundell uses verbs in the opening chapter. Read this passage and listen for the verbs.
Chapter 1: 'Flight'
The pilot frowned and tapped the joystick. He touched the throttle and the plane soared upwards, higher into the clouds. It was almost dark when Fred began to worry.
Teacher note: Read the passage aloud first. Ask students to listen for the verbs before you reveal the annotations on the next card.
Phase 9: I Do
Verb Annotations
Here are the verbs from the 'Flight' passage, classified by type.
Behavioural

frowned = bodily expression showing concern

Physical

tapped = the pilot's action on the joystick

touched = the pilot's action on the throttle

soared = the plane's movement, suggests freedom and power

Mental

worry = Fred's inner fear is introduced

Teacher note: There are mostly physical verbs in this passage because flying a plane is a physical act. The single mental verb at the end (worry) plants the seed of trouble to come.
Phase 10: We Do
The Pilot's Collapse
Copy this passage into your book. We will use it to identify different verb types together.
The pilot began to belch. His hand jerked, and the plane dipped. Someone screamed behind Fred. The plane lurched away from the river and over the canopy. The pilot grunted, gasped and wound back the throttle, slowing the engine. He gave a cough that sounded like a choke. Fred stared.
Teacher note: Give students 3 to 4 minutes to copy this passage into their books. They will need it for the verb quiz on the next card.
Phase 11: We Do
Verb Quiz
What type of verb is this? Come up and tap the correct answer on the board.
Verb 1 of 10
"belch"
Key insight: As the pilot is dying he moves less and less. The children cannot move because they are strapped into their seats. Verbal and behavioural verbs (screams, grunts, gasps, stares) come to the front. The author is using verb choice to show us that the world has shrunk to sound and watching, which is terrifying.
Phase 12: We Do
Discussion
Talk with your partner or table group about these questions.
Discussion Prompts
  1. Why does the author use the verb 'lurched' rather than 'moved'? What feeling does 'lurched' give us?
  2. What changes about the verbs as the passage continues? Why do you think the author makes this change?
  3. How would the passage feel different if we used gentle verbs like 'drifted' and 'whispered' instead?

Justify Your Thinking: Why Does This Verb Create Tension?
Model Response
The verb 'wrenched' creates tension because it shows Fred using force and effort. It is not a gentle word. When you wrench something, you twist it violently. This tells us Fred is desperate and in pain. He is fighting against his discomfort to build the raft. The word 'wrenched' reminds us that the situation is dangerous.
Why this works: It explains the verb choice, connects it to emotion and context, and uses a direct quote from the passage to justify the thinking.
