Complications & Rising Action

What problem must a hero overcome and how does tension build?

Think first

Think about a story you know well. What is the BIG problem the main character has to overcome?

Complication

The complication is the central problem a character faces in a story. It is the reason the story exists.

Rising action

Rising action is the part of the narrative where tension builds. A series of events happens as the character tries to resolve the problem. This is what keeps us reading.

The Three Little Pigs
Three little pigs are building houses. What is the central complication that drives the whole story?
Complication: The wolf wants to eat the pigs.
Rising action: Each pig builds a house from different materials. The wolf huffs and puffs, blowing two houses down, before failing at the brick house.
Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone
Harry arrives at Hogwarts and discovers a magical world. What is the BIG problem he must face?
Complication: Voldemort is trying to return to power by stealing the Philosopher's Stone.
Rising action: Harry, Ron and Hermione uncover clues, pass through trapdoors and obstacles, and finally confront Quirrell hiding Voldemort.
The Lion King
Simba is born a prince. What complication tears his world apart?
Complication: Scar murders Mufasa and tricks Simba into believing it was his fault, forcing Simba into exile.
Rising action: Simba grows up in hiding, meets Nala, learns the truth from Rafiki, and must decide whether to return and reclaim Pride Rock.
Finding Nemo
Marlin is a careful father with one little son. What complication sets the whole journey in motion?
Complication: Nemo is captured by a diver and Marlin must travel across the ocean to find him.
Rising action: Marlin meets Dory, escapes sharks, survives jellyfish, rides currents, and pieces together where Nemo has been taken.
The Wild Robot
A robot called Roz washes up on a wild island. What is the central problem she has to solve?
Complication: Roz must learn how to survive on the island and care for an orphaned gosling, Brightbill.
Rising action: Roz studies the animals, builds a shelter, raises Brightbill, faces a brutal winter, and is hunted by RECO robots sent to retrieve her.

Apply to The Explorer

What is the key complication the characters in The Explorer must overcome?

Discuss with your partner first.

Surviving the jungle after the plane crash, with no adults to help them.

Follow-up: What events so far have built the rising action? List two.

The pattern

Every great story has a central complication and a rising action that builds tension. Today we will look at one of the tools authors use to make that tension feel real on the page: physical verbs.

Verb Types & Modelling on 'Flight'

Recall the four verb types, then watch me highlight them in Chapter 1

Recall

Last term we sorted verbs into four families. Today we will use that same skill to notice how authors choose verbs deliberately to build tension. Watch as I highlight the verbs in Chapter 1, 'Flight'.

RULE 1 / 4
Rule 1
Mental verbs (thinking)
Mental verbs describe what is happening inside a character's head. They show thought, feeling or knowing.
Examples: wondered, remembered, believed, worried, knew
Try it: highlight the mental verbs
Fred wondered whether the pilot was unwell. He thought about his mother and remembered her warm kitchen.
Answer
Fred wondered whether the pilot was unwell. He thought about his mother and remembered her warm kitchen.
Rule 2
Physical verbs (action)
Physical verbs describe a movement of the body or an object. The reader can picture the action happening.
Examples: pushed, soared, jerked, gripped, ran, lurched
Try it: highlight the physical verbs
He touched the throttle and the plane soared upwards, higher into the clouds.
Answer
He touched the throttle and the plane soared upwards, higher into the clouds.
Rule 3
Verbal verbs (speaking)
Verbal verbs describe an utterance, a sound that comes from the mouth to communicate.
Examples: whispered, screamed, gasped, grunted, muttered
Try it: highlight the verbal verbs
Someone screamed behind Fred. The pilot grunted and gasped as he fought with the controls.
Answer
Someone screamed behind Fred. The pilot grunted and gasped as he fought with the controls.
Rule 4
Behavioural verbs (in between)
Behavioural verbs sit between physical and mental. They are bodily actions that show what is going on inside, like laughing, watching, or frowning.
Examples: frowned, laughed, watched, stared, belched
Try it: highlight the behavioural verbs (and any physical ones)
The pilot frowned and tapped the joystick. Fred stared at him while the plane shook.
Answer
The pilot frowned and tapped the joystick. Fred stared at him while the plane shook.

I do: modelling on 'Flight'

The pilot frowned and tapped the joystick. He touched the throttle and the plane soared upwards, higher into the clouds. It was almost dark when Fred began to worry.
The pilot frowned and tapped the joystick. He touched the throttle and the plane soared upwards, higher into the clouds. It was almost dark when Fred began to worry.
Notice: there are mostly physical verbs in this passage because flying a plane is a physical act. The plane is moving and so is the pilot. The single mental verb at the end (worry) plants the seed of trouble to come.

What did you notice?

As the pilot is dying he moves less and less. The children cannot move because they are strapped into their seats. Verbal and behavioural verbs (screams, grunts, gasps, stares) come to the front. The author is using verb choice to show us that the world has shrunk to sound and watching, which is terrifying.

  2. What does the shift from physical verbs to verbal and behavioural verbs tell us about who has control?
  3. If you removed the verb 'lurched' and replaced it with 'moved', what would be lost?

Skim, Sort & Justify: 'The Raft' (p75)

Skim for physical verbs, sort calm vs tense, then justify one choice

Your task

Turn to page 75. Fred has woken with pain in his stomach and decides to build a raft. Tension does not need to be hugely dramatic. There is tension in this scene because building the raft is a significant event for the children's survival.

  • Find the passage on p75
  • Skim for physical verbs (do not read every word)
  • Sort verbs into calm/neutral and tension/danger groups
  • Justify one tense verb in writing

How to skim — reference card

▼

Skimming means letting your eyes move quickly across the page. You do not read every word. You scan for one type of word, in this case physical verbs (action words you can picture).

  • Drag your finger or a pencil down the page to keep your eyes moving
  • Look for the verb after each character or pronoun (he, she, Fred, Con, Lila)
  • Ignore description and dialogue tags for now
  • Write each verb you find in your books as you go
1

Find the passage

Open your novel to page 75. Fred has woken with stomach pain and has decided to build a raft.

2

Skim for physical verbs

Let your eyes move quickly across the page. Record every physical verb you find.

3

Sort into two groups

Sort your verbs into (a) calm or neutral actions and (b) verbs that suggest tension or danger.

4

Justify one choice

Choose one verb that suggests tension. Write what it means, how it contributes to tension, and what it suggests about Fred's situation.

Remember: tension does not have to be life-or-death. Even a quiet, careful action can carry tension if the stakes are high. Building the raft matters because the children need it to survive.

Model response

Here is a model that uses the verb 'wrenched' from p75. Notice how every part of the success criteria is covered.

The verb I chose is wrenched, when Fred wrenches a vine free from the tree. Wrenched means to pull something violently with a sharp, twisting force. It contributes to tension because it is much harsher than a gentle word like pulled or untied; the reader feels the strain in Fred's arms and senses that the jungle is not giving up its materials easily. This suggests Fred's situation is urgent and physically demanding. He is a child doing the work of an adult, alone, with stomach pain, and even the simple act of gathering vines is a battle.
Names the verb and gives the meaning
"wrenched means to pull something violently with a sharp, twisting force"
The success criteria ask the writer to explain what the verb means. A precise definition shows the reader has thought carefully about word choice.
Explains how the verb creates tension
"much harsher than a gentle word like pulled or untied; the reader feels the strain in Fred's arms"
Comparing the chosen verb to a calmer alternative makes the tension visible. The writer shows they understand that authors choose between options.
Connects to Fred's situation in the novel
"He is a child doing the work of an adult, alone, with stomach pain"
Linking the language to the wider story is the highest level of analysis. It proves the writer is reading like a thinker, not just a verb-spotter.
