Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Chapter 3: The Den

Learning intention: We are learning to read closely, build vocabulary, and notice what an author reveals about a character through small details.

Success criteria
  • I can explain the meaning of new vocabulary from the chapter and identify each word's part of speech.
  • I can find details in the text that reveal something about a character beyond what is directly stated.
  • I can create an annotated sketch that shows my understanding of a key setting.
Phase 1 / 7
Phase 2 of 7 · Whole Class

Vocabulary

Words to learn before and during our reading of The Den

trespassing
Verb (present participle)
Entering someone's land or property without permission. In the novel, the children are in a place where they do not belong and have no right to be.
botanist
Noun
A scientist who studies plants. Understanding this word helps us see why a character might know so much about the jungle and what grows there.
grudgingly
Adverb
Doing something reluctantly, without wanting to. When a character acts grudgingly, the author is showing us that they disagree with what they are doing but do it anyway.

Phase 2 / 7
Phase 3 of 7 · Whole Class

Read Aloud: The Den

Teacher reads Chapter 3, stopping at the end of page 24

Read together

Listen carefully as we read Chapter 3 (The Den) aloud, stopping at the end of page 24.

Discussion: 'Con had her thumb in her mouth'

What does this single detail tell you about Con as a character? Think about what it reveals without the author having to explain it directly.

This is a small, physical detail that does a lot of work. Thumb-sucking is something we associate with very young children who need comfort. Con is not a baby, so the fact that she is doing this tells us she is deeply frightened, overwhelmed, or seeking comfort in a situation where there is none. The author does not write 'Con was scared'. Instead, she shows us a gesture and lets us figure it out. This is a strong example of show, don't tell.

Typo hunt (page 24)

There is a tense error on page 24. Can you spot it? The text uses 'does not' when it should say something else.

The text says 'does not' but it should say 'did not'. The rest of the passage is written in past tense, so 'does not' breaks the pattern. Even published books have errors. Good readers notice when something does not sound right.

Phase 4 of 7 · Independent

Read and Sketch the Den

Read pages 25 to 28, then draw what you see

1

Read pages 25 to 28

Read independently. Pay close attention to the description of the den. Notice every detail the author gives you about what it looks like, what is in it, and how it feels.

2

Create an annotated sketch

In your book, draw the den as you picture it from the text. Around the outside of your sketch, add labels and short quotes from the text that support what you have drawn. Your sketch should show that you have read carefully, not that you are a brilliant artist.

3

Finish the chapter

Once your sketch is complete, continue reading to the end of Chapter 3.

Phase 4 / 7
Phase 5 of 7 · Small Groups

Readers Theatre

Bring The Den to life in small groups

What to do

In groups of 4 to 5, use the Readers Theatre scripts for The Den. Each person takes a role. Read the script aloud together, focusing on expression and meaning.

Think about how each character would sound. What are they feeling? How does their voice change as the scene develops?

Where to find the script

Your teacher will share the Readers Theatre script for this chapter. Follow along and mark any words or lines you want to discuss afterwards.

Phase 6 of 7 · Independent

Record and Predict

Update your records and look ahead

Update your Character Development Record

Add any new observations from Chapter 3. What have you learned about the characters from this chapter? Think about what they say, what they do, and what small details the author uses to reveal who they are.

Predict what comes next

The next two chapters are called 'The River' and 'Food (Almost)'. Before you read on, predict what each chapter might contain. What challenges might the children face? What does 'almost' suggest about the food situation?
