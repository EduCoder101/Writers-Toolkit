Learning Intention & Success Criteria

What we are learning and how we will know we have learned it.

Success criteria

PHASE 1 / 6
Phase 2 of 6 · 5 min

Shut or Slammed? Why Word Choice Matters

Discuss how a single verb can transform a character's state of mind, then explore familiar examples.

Think first

Think first

What does the difference between 'shut the door' and 'slammed the door' tell us about a character's state of mind? Talk to the person next to you.

Why word choice matters

Writers choose interesting, more complex vocabulary for a purpose. The right word engages the reader, reveals character, and makes meaning clearer. A weak word like 'shut' tells us what happened. A stronger word like 'slammed' tells us how it happened, and what the character was feeling.

Exemplar 1 · Harry Potter

Harry Potter

Hermione is whispering a warning while the trio sneak through Hogwarts at night. Why does J.K. Rowling write 'hissed' instead of 'said'?
Weak
'Be quiet!' Hermione said.
Strong
'Be quiet!' Hermione hissed.
Hissed turns up the urgency. It tells us Hermione is tense, frightened of being caught, and trying to stay silent. Said is flat, it gives us none of that.
Exemplar 2 · The Lion King

The Lion King

Watch Scar as he approaches Mufasa near the gorge. Which verb does the storyteller choose, and what does it reveal about his plan?
Weak
Scar walked towards Mufasa.
Strong
Scar slunk towards Mufasa.
Slunk shows us a predator. It is sneaky, low to the ground, and full of bad intent. Walked would tell us nothing about who Scar really is.
Exemplar 3 · Matilda

Matilda

Miss Trunchbull is furious that a child has spoken back to her. Why does Roald Dahl write 'bellowed' rather than 'shouted'?
Weak
'Get out!' shouted Miss Trunchbull.
Strong
'Get out!' bellowed Miss Trunchbull.
Bellowed makes her sound monstrous, like a charging bull. It matches the character Dahl is building, a giant, terrifying, almost inhuman headmistress.
Exemplar 4 · The Wild Robot

The Wild Robot

Roz is still learning that the forest is dangerous. Why does Peter Brown choose 'crept' instead of 'moved'?
Weak
Roz moved through the forest.
Strong
Roz crept through the forest.
Crept shows us Roz is learning, becoming careful and aware. It signals that she now understands the wild animals around her are watching. Moved would lose all of that growth.
Exemplar 5 · Wonder

Wonder

Auggie has just been asked if he is okay after a hard day at school. Why does R.J. Palacio choose 'mumbled' over 'said'?
Weak
'I'm fine,' said Auggie.
Strong
'I'm fine,' mumbled Auggie.
Mumbled tells us the opposite of his words. He is not fine at all. The verb does the emotional work, showing his hurt without him having to say it.

Notice the pattern

In every example, the writer could have used a flat verb. Instead they chose one that did extra work: revealing emotion, mood, or character. Today we are going to build the skill of choosing those stronger words on purpose.

How a Word Cline Works

Watch how context shapes word choice, then place words on a scale from weakest to strongest.

Rule 1 of 3
Rule 1: Without context, the 'best' word can't be chosen
When a sentence stands alone, many different words could fit. Without more information, there is no way to know which one is right.
Try it
Tom ________ back. Which word is best: walked, moved, stepped, jumped, leapt, ran?
Answer
Tom ? back. We cannot decide yet, every option could fit. We need more context before we can choose.
Rule 2: Context tells us which word fits the meaning
Once a sentence gives us a reason or a trigger, the right word becomes clear. The context narrows our choice to the verb that carries the right meaning and emotion.
Try it
Tom ________ back. 'Snake!' Now choose from: walked, moved, stepped, jumped, leapt.
Answer
Tom leapt back. 'Snake!' The danger context tells us Tom moved suddenly and far, so 'leapt' fits the meaning.
Rule 3: A word cline is a scale from weakest to strongest
A word cline organises related words on a scale. Weaker, gentler words sit at one end; stronger, more intense words sit at the other. Opinions may differ on the middle, and that is fine.
Try it
Order these verbs about the air from weakest to strongest: chilly, arctic, cool, icy, nippy, freezing, cold.
Answer
cool → chilly → nippy → cold → icy → freezing → arctic (a reasonable order, though students may justify small variations).

Ella's Word Cline

Together, brainstorm word options, add context, then place words on the cline as a class.

Step 1 of 5
Step 1 · Brainstorm without context

Brainstorm without context

Which verbs could fill the gap in 'Ella ____ home.'? Why can't we pick the best one yet?
Ella ________ home.
Teacher note
Accept any reasonable verb (walked, ran, hurried, drove, cycled, dawdled). Draw out the point that without context every option is equally plausible, so no choice is more 'correct' than another.
Step 2 · Add context

Add context

Read the enriched sentence. What does the second sentence tell us about Ella's state of mind?
Ella ________ home. She couldn't wait to see the new puppy.
Teacher note
Lead students to notice that 'couldn't wait' signals excitement and urgency, which rules out slow or reluctant verbs and points to a fast, eager one.
Step 3 · Order the word bank

Order the word bank

Order these verbs from weakest (slowest, most reluctant) to strongest (fastest, most eager).
trudged dawdled hobbled walked hurried raced sprinted
Teacher note
Some pairs are debatable (dawdled vs hobbled). Accept reasoned alternatives but agree on a class order before moving on.
Step 4 · Place words on the cline

Place words on the cline

Place each bank word between the anchors 'trudged' and 'sprinted' on the cline.
trudged sprinted

Step 5 · Read the complete sentence

Read the complete sentence

Read the complete sentence aloud using the verb you think fits the context best.
Ella ________ home. She couldn't wait to see the new puppy.
Ella raced home. She couldn't wait to see the new puppy.
Teacher note
Either 'raced' or 'sprinted' is defensible. 'Hurried' is a softer alternative. Slow verbs (trudged, dawdled, hobbled) clash with 'couldn't wait' and should be ruled out.

Build Your Own Word Clines

Create semantic gradients for four word groups taken from 'The Raft'.

Your task

Use the four word groups from 'The Raft' below. For each group, place the words on a cline from weakest to strongest. Then write one sentence using the word you think fits best with a clear context.

  • Order each group from weakest to strongest.
  • Write one sentence with context for the verb you choose.
  • Be ready to justify your placement.
Support: your teacher will give you the lowest and highest anchor words for each cline. Place the rest in between.
Group 1
Chewed / nibbled / pecked / devoured / gobbled
Group 2
Cascaded / poured / trickled
Group 3
Rubbed / scoured / dabbed / patted / erased
Group 4
Pulling / yanking / stretching / removing / taking

Need a model? See one student's worked example

▼

Here is one student's worked example for the cline: cascaded / poured / trickled.

Model response

Cline (weakest → strongest):

trickled → poured → cascaded

Sentence in context:

After the storm, fresh water cascaded down the rocks, drumming onto the leaves Fred had stretched across the raft.

Anchored the ends first: I started by placing 'trickled' at the weakest end and 'cascaded' at the strongest. Anchors make the middle easier to judge.
Picked the middle by force: 'Poured' sits between because it is faster than a trickle but does not have the dramatic surge that 'cascaded' suggests.
Matched the verb to the moment: I used 'cascaded' in my sentence because the storm provides the context. A trickle would feel wrong after heavy rain in the rainforest.

Challenge

Create your own word cline using verbs you find in 'The Raft' chapter. Then, write a procedural text giving instructions for how to construct a raft, including materials and sequenced steps.
