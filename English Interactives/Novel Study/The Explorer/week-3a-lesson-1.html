Learning Intention and Success Criteria

What we are learning today and how we will know we have got there

Learning intention: We are learning to build field knowledge and use it to deepen our comprehension of a text.

Success criteria
  • I can notice the difference between how a character is labelled on the surface and what the text actually shows about them.
  • I can use facts about the real world (like piranhas) to read a story scene with sharper understanding.
  • I can identify a writer's technique in a single line and explain its effect on the reader.

Phase 2 of 8 · 5 min

The Person on the Page vs the Person Inside

How a single label rarely captures who someone really is

Before we begin

Turn to a partner. What words have your school reports or teachers used to describe you? If you were writing your own report, would you choose the same words, or different ones? Why?

The label vs the person

A description, a label, or a first impression rarely captures the whole person. Authors know this. They often give us a surface description (what others say) and then a deeper one (what the character actually feels, wants, or is).

We are going to look at four characters you already know and notice the gap between the label they are given and who they really are.

Harry Potter
The Dursleys describe Harry as 'a nasty, troublesome boy'. What does the rest of the story show us about who Harry actually is?
Surface label
A nuisance. A burden. 'Just like his good-for-nothing parents.'
Deeper truth
A loyal, brave boy who carries a story bigger than the cupboard under the stairs. The label tells us about the Dursleys, not about Harry.
Matilda
Matilda's parents call her 'a noisy chatterbox' and 'a nuisance'. What does Roald Dahl show us instead?
Surface label
A nuisance child who reads too much and asks too many questions.
Deeper truth
A brilliant, sharp, kind girl whose mind is years ahead of the adults around her. The label says more about her parents' smallness than about Matilda.

Phase 3 of 8 · 5 min

Spotting the Pattern

Two more characters, then the rule that connects them all

Wonder (Auggie)
Strangers describe Auggie by his face before they describe anything else. What does R. J. Palacio show us about who he really is?
Surface label
A face people stare at. A boy judged before he speaks.
Deeper truth
Funny, clever, loyal, ordinary in all the ways that matter. The label is everyone else's first reaction, not Auggie.
The Wild Robot (Roz)
The animals first call Roz 'a monster' and 'just a machine'. What does Peter Brown show us underneath?
Surface label
A cold, dangerous machine that does not belong on the island.
Deeper truth
A character who learns, protects, and loves. The label is about fear of what is unfamiliar, not about who Roz becomes.

The pattern to notice

Every one of these characters has a gap between the label they are given and who they really are. Good readers learn to spot that gap. Today, we are going to look for the same gap in someone in our novel: Fred.

Phase 4 of 8 · 10 min

Surface vs Depth: Looking Closely at Fred

Compare the labels Fred is given with what the text actually shows us

Three revealing moments

We are going to look at three moments where what Fred is called, or what Con first thinks, does not match what the text actually shows. Each pair reveals a small but important truth about who Fred really is.

Pair 1: The report vs the narrator
What the report says
Fred is described in his school report as 'sensible'.
What the narrator shows us
'... hunger and hope and wire ... Fred's mind was quick, with sharp edges. He wanted more from the world than it had yet given.'
The report uses one tidy word: sensible. The narrator gives us three: hunger, hope, wire. The report flattens Fred. The narrator shows a boy who is restless, hungry for more, and a little dangerous in his sharpness. A 'sensible' boy does not usually end up in a plane crash over the Amazon. The author is hinting that Fred is exactly the kind of person who would.
Pair 2: First impression vs reality
Con's first impression
Con assumes Fred is older, almost an adult, someone who can take charge and get them out of this.
The reality Con realises
Fred is not older. He is just tall for his age. He is a child like she is.
This is the moment the safety net disappears. Con had been quietly leaning on the idea that there was an adult in the situation. Realising Fred is just tall, not old, makes her feel more alone, more exposed, more in danger. The author uses a small, physical fact (height) to deliver a huge emotional shift (no one is coming to save us).
Pair 3: Outward calm vs inner hunger
What Fred shows on the outside
Fred appears calm, capable, organised. The kind of boy a teacher would write 'sensible' about.
What Fred wants on the inside
He 'wanted more from the world than it had yet given.'
Underneath the calm is a quiet, burning ambition. Fred is not sensible at all in the deepest part of himself. He is hungry. The river, the jungle, the crash, this whole impossible situation, may turn out to be the very thing that finally gives Fred 'more'. The author is setting Fred up to become something the report could never have predicted.

Readers Theatre

In pairs, read the line 'hunger and hope and wire' three different ways. Once as a whisper, once as a warning, once as a promise. Which reading best matches the Fred you are starting to see?

Write Your Own Semester 1 General Comment

If you wrote your own report, what would it say?

Your task

Write your own General Comment for Semester 1, exactly as a teacher might write it on a school report. Use full sentences. Try to be honest, not just kind to yourself, and not just hard on yourself. Aim for the version of you the report rarely captures.

Scaffold (open if you need a structure)

  2. Give two specific examples from this term that back that up.
  3. End with one sentence about what you are working towards next.

Write your General Comment

▲

Extension

Phase 6 of 8 · 12 min

Building Field Knowledge: Red-Bellied Piranhas

Get the facts before we feel the threat in the river

Why we are stopping for facts

In a moment, the word 'Piranhas!' is going to land in the middle of our chapter. If we do not know what a piranha actually is, the moment passes us by. Real-world knowledge makes the fiction hit harder. This is what 'building field knowledge' means.

1

Watch the clip

Watch the Red-Bellied Piranhas video together. As you watch, listen for: how they hunt, how big they get, and what they actually do (and do not do) to humans.

2

Read the article

Open the National Geographic Kids article on Red-Bellied Piranhas (posted to GCR). Read it carefully.

3

Record 5 key points

In your books, record 5 key points about red-bellied piranhas. Write each as a full sentence. These are the facts you will carry into the next part of the chapter.

Carry it back to the page

Now, when we return to page 47 and read 'Piranhas!', you will not just hear a scary word. You will hear a real animal, in a real river, doing exactly what you have just learned it does. That is field knowledge deepening comprehension.

Phase 7 of 8 · 5 min

'Hope. Either That, or Concussion.'

Analyse Fred's final thought from the chapter

'It felt like hope. Either that, or concussion.'

Read Fred's final thought of the chapter. What does it mean? What technique is the writer using, and what does it do for us as readers?

  • Understatement. Fred has just survived a plane crash. To compare hope to concussion is to make a huge feeling small, almost a joke.
  • Humour. The line is funny. It tells us Fred copes by making light of terrible things. That is character.
  • Abstract noun. 'Hope' is the abstract noun. The author pairs it with a concrete, physical word ('concussion') to make hope feel almost touchable.
  • Simile-like comparison. 'Felt like' invites us to weigh one feeling against another. We finish the chapter not sure which it is, and that uncertainty is exactly the point.
