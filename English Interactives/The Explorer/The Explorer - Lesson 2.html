Recap & cloze

We explored the opening last lesson. Now make it your own by choosing new words that still make sense.

Learning intention A: We are learning to make textual predictions and substitute words carefully so a passage still makes sense.

Learning intention B: We are learning to review narrative structure and to identify the tone a writer sets in the opening of a story.

Success criteria
  • I can complete a cloze passage using context clues, grammar and my understanding of the text.
  • I can identify the tone a writer sets in the opening of a story.
  • I can name the key parts of a narrative and put them in order on Freytag's pyramid.
  • I can record the meaning of new words from chapter 2 and sort them by how well I know them.
  • I can notice what a character says or does and interpret what this reveals about them.

What is a cloze?

A cloze passage uses the skeleton of the original text but asks you to substitute words. Your choices need to make sense in the sentence and in the story.

You are not creating something wildly different. For example, Katherine Rundell's title, The Explorer, could become The Adventurer. It would not become The Unicorn Rider because that is wildly different from the story.

Story openings & tone

Opening sentences orient the reader. They set the mood, the setting and the writer's attitude: the tone.

"Where's Papa going with that axe?"

Charlotte's Web, by E. B. White
Tone: Alarming, ominous
Starts with a question about violence (an axe). We are immediately worried. The writer pulls us in with a tiny shock.

"If you are interested in stories with happy endings, you would be better off reading some other book."

The Bad Beginning, by Lemony Snicket
Tone: Dry, warning, playful
The narrator speaks directly to us in a serious voice about unhappy things. That contrast is funny and unsettling at the same time.

"Our story begins on the ocean, with wind and rain and thunder and lightning and waves."

The Wild Robot, by Peter Brown
Tone: Dramatic, epic
The repeated "and...and...and..." builds up like waves crashing. The scale feels huge. Something serious is about to happen.

What tone does The Explorer set?

Look again at Katherine Rundell's opening: "Like a man-made magic wish, the aeroplane began to rise."

What tone has the writer set? How does it make you feel as a reader?

The writer sets an admiring, optimistic, slightly awed tone. "Magic wish" treats the plane as something wonderful. "Began to rise" is gentle and hopeful. We feel uplifted. Katherine Rundell wants us to love this aeroplane before anything bad happens to it.

Why "The Green Dark"?

Look at the chapter heading. Why do you think the author, Katherine Rundell, chose those three words for this chapter? Discuss in pairs.

"Green" is the colour of the thick jungle canopy. "Dark" tells us danger is coming. Put together, the two words make the jungle feel both beautiful and frightening at once.

Character development

A character's words and actions show us who they are. Watch for the clues.

How do writers reveal a character?

As you listen to chapter 2, notice clues about the four children. A clue might be:

  • something a character says,
  • something a character does,
  • or something another character says about them.

Worked example

CharacterCon
(Constanza)
Says / does"Left at the tree that was covered in mushrooms, and then right at the bush with green thorns." (p41)
What this revealsCon has a photographic memory. She notices tiny details and can remember the route she has walked.

The pattern: quote or action → interpretation. What does this behaviour tell us about who the character is?

Fred English, around 11, on his way back to boarding school
"He jabbed his finger into the back of his tongue to summon up spit."
Reveals: Fred is determined and practical under pressure. He does a strange, unpleasant thing because he needs to, not because he wants to. That is a sign of resilience.
"He grabbed a rock from the ground and hurled it in the direction of the voice."
Reveals: Fred reacts first and thinks later. He is frightened but not passive. His instinct is to defend himself.
Con (Constanza) Was on the aeroplane with Fred
"For God's sake, don't throw things!"
Reveals: Con is direct and not easily pushed around. Even scared, she speaks up. The exclamation shows she is firm.
"Please don't hurt us," said the bush.
Reveals: She is also cautious and protective. She says "us" not "me", so she is thinking about someone else, not just herself.
Lila Another child from the plane
"The accent wasn't British. It was something softer."
Reveals: Lila is not from the same place as Fred. The word "softer" hints at a gentler way of speaking and, perhaps, a gentler character.
"Yes, we're hurt. We fell out of the sky."
Reveals: Lila tells the plain truth, even when the other voice is trying to hide how hurt they are. She is honest.
Max The youngest of the group
"No, not badly." (as the other said they were hurt)
Reveals: Max is brave or does not want to complain. He might also be too young to realise how hurt he is. Watch for more clues as the chapter goes on.
