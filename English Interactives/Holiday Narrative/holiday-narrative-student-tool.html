Prompt 1 of 4

Not a whole town. Just one place. A room, a corner of a beach, the back seat of a car. What did it look like? What could you smell?

Lens 1 of 6

Same moment, different lens

Simile
The wave rose up like a green wall and crashed onto the beach.
What the lens does: Compares using like or as. Lets the reader picture something familiar.
Lens 2 of 6
Metaphor
The wave was a green wall, and it collapsed onto the beach.
What the lens does: Says one thing is another. Stronger and more direct than a simile.
Lens 3 of 6
Personification
The wave reached for the beach, then let go of everything it had been carrying.
What the lens does: Gives a non-human thing human qualities. Makes the world feel alive.
Lens 4 of 6
Hyperbole
The wave was taller than my house, and the crash shook the entire planet.
What the lens does: Deliberate exaggeration. Useful for emphasis or humour.
Lens 5 of 6
Onomatopoeia
The wave reared up and WHOOOSH — crash, hiss, gurgle — onto the beach.
What the lens does: Words that imitate the sound. Pulls the reader's ears into the scene.
Lens 6 of 6
Idiom
The wave came out of the blue and crashed onto the beach.
What the lens does: A familiar saying with a non-literal meaning. Use sparingly.
Watch out: Idiom, hyperbole and onomatopoeia are easy to overdo. If every sentence is exaggerated, nothing feels exaggerated.

Here's the thing

Everything we've just done was planning.

The chat with your partner. The words on the board. The wave on the beach.

You didn't realise it, but you've already done the hardest part of writing a story: you've found a moment, you've thought about how it looked and sounded and felt, and you've reminded yourselves of six tools that good writers use.

So now write me a short narrative based on a moment from your holidays.

It can be true, or made-up, or true at the start and made-up at the end. What matters is that it's a moment, not a recount of the whole holiday. Show me ten seconds. Make me feel them.

Your writing brief

Length
One full page, or 200–300 words
Time
35–40 minutes
Form
Narrative, not recount
Draft in
Pencil, cross-outs welcome

What we're learning

Learning Intention

We are learning to craft a short narrative that captures a single vivid moment, using sensory detail, figurative language and varied sentence openers to draw a reader into the experience.

Success Criteria

  • I have chosen one moment rather than retelling a whole event.
  • I have used at least two different types of figurative language on purpose.
  • I have varied my sentence openers so the writing does not begin every sentence with I or Then.
  • I have shown the reader the setting and mood through what I see, hear, smell or feel.
  • My writing has a clear beginning, middle and end, even though it is only a moment.
