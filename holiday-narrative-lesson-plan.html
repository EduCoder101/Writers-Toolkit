A Moment from the Holidays

A narrative writing sample lesson built around a single moment from the holidays.

Stage 3 (Year 5–6) NSW English K–10 Syllabus Approx. 90 minutes total Roseville College

At a glance

Goal
A narrative writing sample
Stimulus
A holiday moment (real or imagined)
Form
Narrative, not recount
Focus
A snippet, an instance, a moment

Learning Intention & Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to craft a short narrative that captures a single vivid moment, using sensory detail, figurative language and varied sentence openers to draw a reader into the experience.

Success Criteria

  • I have chosen one moment rather than retelling a whole event.
  • I have used at least two different types of figurative language on purpose.
  • I have varied my sentence openers so the writing does not begin every sentence with I or Then.
  • I have shown the reader the setting and mood through what I see, hear, smell or feel.
  • My writing has a clear beginning, middle and end, even though it is only a moment.

01

The Holiday Chat

20 minutes Pair-share then whole-class harvest No mention of writing yet

This is the secret planning phase. The girls believe they are simply chatting about their holidays. You are quietly gathering raw material: setting, character, sensory detail, mood. The trick is to keep it warm and conversational, never note-taking-ish.

The framing Open with something genuinely casual. Tell them about a small moment from your own holidays first, something tiny and specific. The smell of sunscreen on the back of your hand. The sound of cicadas as you waited for an Uber. The exact moment your dog realised you were home. Modelling the smallness of the moment is the most important thing you do all lesson.

Step 1 · Teacher anecdote (3 min)

Share your own micro-moment. Make it ordinary. Something with sensory texture but no plot. The point is to reset their expectations away from "tell me everything we did on the holidays."

Step 2 · Pair-share (6 min)

Put the girls in pairs (table partners is fine). Project the prompt cards below one at a time, giving 60–90 seconds for each. They take turns. Both partners speak.

Prompt 1 · Setting Tell your partner about a place you spent time in over the holidays. Not a whole town, just one place. A room, a corner of a beach, a back seat of a car. What did it look like? What could you smell?
Prompt 2 · Character Was there a person who said something that stuck in your head? Or a stranger you noticed for some reason? Or a pet that did something funny? Tell your partner about them in detail.
Prompt 3 · A moment Think of a moment that lasted maybe ten seconds. The moment something tipped over, or the cake came out, or you realised something, or the lights went off. Tell your partner what was happening just before, during and just after.
Prompt 4 · Mood How did the holidays feel overall? Pick one word. Not "good" or "fun." Push for something more interesting: peaceful, restless, hot, slow, bright, lonely, full.

Step 3 · Whole-class harvest (10 min)

Bring them back. Don't ask "who would like to share?" because you'll get the same three hands. Instead, ask in this order:

  1. "Hands up if your partner said something you thought was really specific or interesting." Choose two girls to share their partner's moment. This raises the floor of what counts as worth sharing.
  2. "Did anyone's holiday have a really clear smell?" Then sound. Then a colour. Cycle through the senses.
  3. "Did anyone have a moment that felt slow when it happened?" or "Was there a moment where everything went quiet?"

As they share, write key words on the board in clusters: Settings, People, Moments, Moods. Don't sort or label too neatly. The board is a holding pen, not a worksheet.

Watch out for The recount drift. Some girls will start telling you the whole story of their family trip. Gently redirect: "Hold that thought, but can you give me just the one second when the boat tipped?" You're training them to zoom in, repeatedly, throughout the chat.
02

Figurative Language Refresher

15 minutes One mentor sentence, six lenses Worked examples on the board

Last term the girls covered six types: simile, metaphor, personification, hyperbole, onomatopoeia and idiom. Rather than re-teach the definitions abstractly, this phase shows how a single ordinary holiday moment can be reworked through each lens. The pedagogical move is: we are not learning new tools, we are watching what they do.

The mentor moment

Project this sentence on the board:

A wave came in and crashed onto the beach.

Ask the girls: is this sentence wrong? Get them to agree: no, it's clear, it's accurate. Then ask: is it good? Push them to articulate why not. (It's flat. It tells you what happened but not what it felt like. It could be any wave on any beach.)

The transformations

Now show them the same moment through each of the six lenses. Reveal one row at a time. After each row, ask: what does this version make you feel that the original doesn't?

Type The wave moment, transformed What the lens does
Simile The wave rose up like a green wall and crashed onto the beach. Compares using like or as. Lets the reader picture something familiar.
Metaphor The wave was a green wall, and it collapsed onto the beach. Says one thing is another. Stronger and more direct than a simile.
Personification The wave reached for the beach, then let go of everything it had been carrying. Gives a non-human thing human qualities. Makes the world feel alive.
Hyperbole The wave was taller than my house, and the crash shook the entire planet. Deliberate exaggeration. Useful for emphasis or humour.
Onomatopoeia The wave reared up and WHOOOSH—crash, hiss, gurgle—onto the beach. Words that imitate the sound. Pulls the reader's ears into the scene.
Idiom The wave came out of the blue and crashed onto the beach. A familiar saying with a non-literal meaning. Use sparingly.
Discussion question "If you could only use two of these in your own writing today, which two would do the most work for the kind of moment you're thinking about?" This question quietly asks them to start matching tools to their own emerging story, without naming the writing task yet.
Watch out for Idiom and hyperbole are the easiest to overdo. Flag this gently: "If every sentence is exaggerated, nothing feels exaggerated." Same with onomatopoeia: one well-placed sound is more powerful than three crammed in.
03

The Reveal & The Resource

10 minutes Pivot to writing Distribute sentence starter sheet

This is the moment of the friendly trick. The girls discover that the chat and the lesson have been planning all along.

Suggested teacher script

"Right. Here's the thing. Everything we've just done—the chat with your partner, the words on the board, the wave on the beach—that was all planning. You didn't realise it, but you've already done the hardest part of writing a story: you've found a moment, you've thought about how it looked and sounded and felt, and you've reminded yourselves of six tools that good writers use. So now I'd like you to write me a short narrative based on a moment from your holidays. It can be true, or it can be made-up, or it can be true at the start and made-up at the end. I don't mind. What I do mind is that it's a moment, not a recount of the whole holiday. Show me ten seconds. Make me feel them."

Distribute the resource

Hand out the Narrative Writer's Toolkit sheet (separate file, see below). It contains:

  • A bank of narrative sentence starters drawn from three categories: passage of time (Instantly, Before long, As the sun set), complex constructions (Although, Despite the fact that, Without noticing that), and changing focus (Suddenly, Meanwhile, Some distance away).
  • A small figurative language reminder with one example per type, in case they need to peek.
  • A self-check strip at the bottom mirroring the success criteria.
Why the resource matters The sentence starter bank does two things: it gives reluctant writers a starting point that isn't I or Then, and it gives confident writers a target to deliberately work towards (use at least three different categories). It is a scaffold and a stretch tool simultaneously.

Set the writing parameters

  • Length: One full page handwritten, or roughly 200–300 words. Short enough to be a moment, long enough to show craft.
  • Time: 35–40 minutes of independent writing.
  • Planning permitted: Three to five minutes of jot-down planning is fine, but no formal story mountain. The chat was the plan.
  • Drafting: First draft in pencil. They can cross out and revise but they don't need to copy out a "good copy."
04

Writing & Light-touch Share

40–45 minutes Independent writing Optional 5-min share at the end

While they write

Resist the urge to over-conference. The point of this lesson is a writing sample, so the writing needs to be theirs. Do circulate, but limit yourself to:

  • A quiet thumbs-up over the shoulder of a girl who has chosen a small moment well.
  • A whisper in the ear of a girl who has drifted into recount: "Where's your ten seconds? Find them."
  • A pointer to the sentence starter sheet for any girl whose first three sentences all begin with I.
Watch out for The blank-page freeze. If a girl is staring at the page after five minutes, don't ask "what are you writing about?" (too big). Ask: "What did your partner say in the pair-share that you remembered most?" Most freezes thaw with that question.

Optional share (5 min, only if time)

Two or three girls read just the opening sentence of their narrative aloud. Not the whole piece. Just the opening. The class listens for: did the opening drop us into the moment, or did it warm up to it?

Collection & assessment

Collect all writing. This is your sample. Use the checklist below as a quick triage when marking, not as a formal rubric for the girls.

Look for What strong evidence looks like
Moment, not recount The narrative covers a tightly bounded slice of time (seconds to minutes), not a full day or trip.
Sensory detail At least two of the five senses are evoked. Specific images rather than generic ones.
Figurative language Two or more types used purposefully. Not just decoratively, but doing work in the sentence.
Sentence opener variety Openings include time connectives, subordinating conjunctions, or focus-shifters. Not all "I" or "Then."
Voice The piece sounds like the writer, not a generic "school story." A reader could pick it as hers.
Structure Clear opening, middle and ending, even within the small moment. Lands somewhere.

Differentiation & Go Deeper

For during writing Optional

For girls who need more support

  • Suggest they begin with the setting first: describe the place for two sentences before anything happens. This often unlocks the moment.
  • Offer a sentence frame: "It was the moment when ___. I could ___ and ___, and the ___ felt ___."
  • Permit them to use the wave-on-the-beach mentor sentence as a starting line, then take it somewhere of their own.

Go Deeper, for confident writers

  • The reverse-order challenge: write the moment, then add one paragraph before it that hints at what is about to happen, without giving it away.
  • The single-sense constraint: write the entire moment using only sound and touch. No sight, no sight-words.
  • The double-perspective version: rewrite the same ten seconds from the point of view of someone else who was there. What would they have noticed that you didn't?

Print and prep before the lesson
